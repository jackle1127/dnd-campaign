# Fantasy Lore Writing - Sample Passages

Annotated examples for reference. Use these to calibrate tone and technique when writing campaign content.

---

## Dark Souls - Armor of Artorias (FromSoftware, 2011)

> "Armor of Artorias the Abysswalker, one of Gwyn's four knights. The death of the armor's owner can be surmised from the corrosive Dark of the Abyss, and the tattered azure-blue cape, once a symbol of pride and glory."

**Why it works:**
- "can be surmised" - the narrative voice never states the death directly; it implies it through physical evidence, like a detective reading a crime scene
- "once a symbol of pride and glory" - five words that do all the tragic work; no emotion is named
- The cape appears in the helm description too; repetition makes it a motif, not a detail
- The structure is: establish greatness, then break it

---

## Elden Ring - Remembrance of the Omen King (FromSoftware, 2022)

> "Though born one of the graceless Omen, Morgott took it upon himself to become the Erdtree's protector. He loved not in return, for he was never loved, but nevertheless, love it he did."

**Why it works:**
- "loved not in return, for he was never loved" - cause and effect collapsed into a single clause; no explanation needed
- "love it he did" - archaic inversion slows the reader and makes "love" land twice in four words
- The whole character's tragedy is compressed to three clauses; nothing is wasted

## Elden Ring - Remembrance of the Starscourge (FromSoftware, 2022)

> "The Red Lion General wielded gravitational powers which he learned in Sellia during his younger days. All so he would never have to abandon his beloved but scrawny steed."

**Why it works:**
- "scrawny" is the key word; a god mastered gravity to spare a *scrawny* horse - the specificity of that detail makes the devotion completely real
- The cosmic (gravitational mastery) and the intimate (a small horse) collide; that collision is the entire characterization
- No explanation of the emotion; the contrast does all the work

## The Name of the Wind - Prologue (Patrick Rothfuss, 2007)

> "It was the patient, cut-flower sound of a man who is waiting to die."

**Why it works:**
- Synesthesia: a sound that describes a state, describing a man
- "cut-flower" - separated from roots, still beautiful, already dying, unaware; that's an entire emotional biography in a hyphenated word
- "patient" signals acceptance, not despair; the horror is that he has made peace with it
- This sentence closes the prologue and also closes the novel; the structural repetition transforms it from image to elegy

## Curse of Strahd - The Tome of Strahd (D&D 5e, 2016)

> "I am the Ancient. I am the Land. My beginnings are lost in the darkness of the past."
>
> "I was the warrior, I was good and just. I thundered across the land like the wrath of a just god, but the war years and the killing years wore down my soul as the wind wears stone into sand. All goodness slipped from my life. I found my youth and strength gone, and all I had left was death."

**Why it works:**
- First-person confession with no self-pity and no justification - the refusal to explain is more frightening than any explanation
- "the war years and the killing years" - the repetition signals that these were different things; war was one thing, killing was another
- "as the wind wears stone into sand" - a slow, geologic metaphor for moral erosion; it happened over a very long time
- The final sentence doesn't reach for drama: "all I had left was death" - it lands like a man reading his own ledger

## Mistborn - Alendi's Journal (Brandon Sanderson, 2006)

> "I never wanted to be feared. If I regret one thing, it is the fear I have caused. Fear is the tool of tyrants. Unfortunately, when the fate of the world is in question, you use whatever tools are available."

**Why it works:**
- This was written by the man who became the eternal tyrant villain of the book - the reader doesn't know this while reading it
- The passage reads as sympathetic, even noble; when the truth lands, it retroactively becomes ironic tragedy
- "you use whatever tools are available" - the line that breaks the reader in hindsight; it sounds like pragmatism until you know what tools he used
- The technique: dramatic irony built across an entire novel, resolved in a single re-read of the epigraph

## Planescape: Torment (Black Isle Studios, 1999)

> "Time is not your enemy. Forever is."

**Why it works:**
- The voice of someone who has had centuries to arrive at this conclusion
- Compression: an entire arc of immortality's horror in seven words
- The twist is syntactic: "forever" is a word associated with hope; here it is the threat

## Key Techniques Summary

| Technique | Example | Apply when |
|-----------|---------|-----------|
| Was/is gap | "once a symbol of pride and glory" | Any object, trait, or relationship that has changed |
| Strangely specific detail | "scrawny steed" | Whenever you want to make abstract devotion or loss feel real |
| Consequence without cause | "can be surmised from the corrosive Dark" | Introducing mystery; backstory that doesn't over-explain |
| First-person confession | Strahd's Tome | Villain backstories, journals, letters found in-world |
| Archaic inversion | "love it he did" | When you want a word to land harder; use sparingly |
| Synesthetic image | "cut-flower sound" | Emotional climax moments; use once per passage maximum |
| Dramatic irony | Alendi's epigraphs | When the reader knows more than the writer of the document |
| Compression | "Time is not your enemy. Forever is." | Closing lines; summaries that open into larger meaning |
