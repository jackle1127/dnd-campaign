# Fantasy Lore Writing - Style Guide

Distilled from Dark Souls, Elden Ring, Rothfuss, Planescape: Torment, Curse of Strahd, and Mistborn. See `sample-passages.md` for annotated examples.

---

## The Fundamental Principle

Lore is not information delivery. Lore is *world felt through language*. The difference between a character bio and a character backstory is the difference between a Wikipedia article and a wound. The best lore doesn't tell you what happened - it makes you feel the weight of what is gone.

---

## DOs

**Anchor the abstract in a single strange physical detail.**
The scrawny horse. The azure-blue tassel. The half-wheel wound. The cut-flower sound. Pick one image that is oddly specific and let it carry the emotional freight. Strange specificity is more memorable than beautiful generality. "His sword was magnificent" is nothing. "His sword was made from the soul of the wolf who still guards his grave" is everything.

**Write from the aftermath, not the event.**
Never describe the battle. Describe the corroded helm afterward. The most powerful temporal position for lore is the moment after everything has already ended. Work backward from wreckage.

**Use the gap between *was* and *is* as your primary tool.**
"Once a symbol of pride and glory" performs this in four words. Establish what something was. Show what it is now. Never explain what happened between. That gap is where the reader lives.

**Let the voice be the character.**
A character's lore should sound like it was written by the person it describes, or found in their possession, or stained with their history. Generic omniscient narrator voice flattens everything.

**Vary sentence length aggressively.**
Short. Then longer, building with appositional weight, layering detail upon detail until the sentence earns its period. Then short again. The rhythm should feel like breathing - constrained and released.

**Lean into a single tone and commit.**
Elegiac, confessional, matter-of-fact, philosophical - pick one and hold it. Mixing tones destroys weight unless the contrast is itself the point.

**Use motif - repeat key images or phrases across sections.**
When an image appears the second time, the reader feels it differently. Repetition transforms a detail into a symbol.

**Embed lore in objects, documents, or personal voices.**
A sword description, a journal entry, a letter, a prophecy fragment, an epitaph - any frame that has its own reason to exist removes the exposition problem.

**Let questions be answers.**
A well-posed question about a world pulls the reader into active engagement. Not every mystery needs resolution.

**Use archaic or slightly unusual syntax for weight.**
"Love it he did" rather than "he loved it nonetheless." The inverted construction slows the reader and makes the key word land harder. Slightly formal syntax makes prose feel like it has existed for a long time.

---

## DON'Ts

**Don't explain the tragedy. Show the texture of it.**
If you write "this was a great tragedy," you have killed the tragedy. Show the tattered cape. Let the reader feel it themselves. Every word spent naming the emotion is a word stolen from the reader's imagination.

**Don't frontload the context.**
Drop the reader into the specific detail. Context emerges. Trust the reader to follow.

**Don't make every detail specific.**
Three precise details are worth more than twenty accurate ones. Choose the details that carry emotional or thematic weight, not the ones that are merely true.

**Don't summarize a character's emotional journey.**
"He had suffered greatly and it had made him hard." That's nothing. Find the specific incident - not the category (suffering) but the thing. Then trust the incident to convey the conclusion without stating it.

**Don't give the cause before the effect.**
Start with the effect and let the reader ask why. Work forward from mystery, not backward from explanation.

**Don't use passive emotional vocabulary.**
Words like "tragic," "heroic," "terrible," "beautiful," "sad" are instructions to feel rather than experiences of feeling. Cut them. Replace each with an image or action that produces the emotion directly.

**Don't stop the world to explain the world.**
If a paragraph exists only to deliver lore and has no other reason to be present - no voice, no implied history, no stylistic interest - it reads as exposition and feels like homework.

**Don't resolve the mystery the lore creates.**
The moment you explain the mystery in full, you've transferred the world from the reader's imagination to the page. The page is smaller than imagination. Leave room.

---

## A Note on Length

The most effective lore passages are almost all under 100 words. Compression forces selectivity. When you can only say three things, you choose the three that matter most. Write at full length for your own understanding, then cut to the bone for the final version. What survives the cutting is the lore.

## Tone Reference

| Tone | How it works | When to use |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| Matter-of-fact melancholy | No narrative voice expressing grief - grief is in the gap between what was and what is | Item descriptions, NPC death notes, artifact histories |
| Confessional | First person, self-aware about loss but not evil, no self-pity, no justification | Personal journals, villain backstories |
| Elegiac/mythic | Unafraid to be poetic, synesthetic images, structural repetition | Major character backstories, world-ending events |
| Philosophical | Dark but laced with gallows humor, moves between cosmic and intimate | Ancient characters, characters who've lost everything and kept going |
| Grounded/interior | Reads like private thoughts, not performance - makes tragic reveals land as real tragedy | Epistolary lore, journals, letters |
